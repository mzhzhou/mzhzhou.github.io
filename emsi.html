Introduction
This project was initiated from thinking about what it means to use space, and what spatial interaction implies. How has our understanding and data collection of how humans use space changed since the work of William H. Whyte, for example? While usability of projects are able to be evaluated in fields such as HCI and industrial design, architecture is limited by scale and costs, and thus these studies can only be done through simulations or through post-occupancy studies from built precedents. In order to experience architecture, a person needs to move around, navigate, and explore in order to obtain a complete impression. Thus, movement becomes the primary way in which we interact with the built environment.
How might we measure this? One of the main complications with understanding how and what people experience, is how highly subjective experiences can be. This project hypothesizes that analysing human motion patterns would be an important contributor towards research in understanding the usability and quality of spaces.
This study explores the systematic, quantitative analysis of motion in a given environment. It attempts to taxonomize human body motion, presenting a framework that can be built upon and layered to represent a wider context of determinants.
Background
During the early 1900s, Lillian and Frank Gilbreth introduced ideas of motion economy through using chronocyclegraphs, where small light bulbs where attached to the tips of worker’s fingers. In their studies, they revealed 18 types of elemental motions in the industrial workplace which were eventually used to identify costly motion within manufacturing and assembly. We drew inspiration from this framework as a starting point to think about how movement can be broken down, in the absence of clear tasks.
EMSI Framework
The EMSI (Elemental Motion in Spatial Interaction) Framework is a response to questions of identifying and understanding movements within built spaces. Broadly, it considers a single input, a human action, and two levels of output. There are four taxonomy groups, State and Type, which allows a computer to firstly identify the movement, and Goal and Texture, which further speculate on how a computer could understand the intent and quality of motion.
